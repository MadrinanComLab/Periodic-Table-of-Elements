Characteristics

Rhenium is one of the transition elements, which range from metals to metal-like elements. Its chemical and physical properties are similar to those of technetium, which is above it in the periodic table. It is not very reactive. When small amounts are added to molybdenum, it forms a unique type of semiconducting metal. It is also noncorrosive in seawater.

Abundance and Source

Rhenium is the 78th most common element found on Earth, which makes it somewhat rare. During the early twentieth century, it required the processing of about a 1,000 pounds of earth to secure just one pound of rhenium, resulting in a price of about $10,000 per gram. Thus, there were few uses for rhenium. Later in the century, improved mining and refining techniques reduced the price. Today, the United States produces about 1,000 pounds of rhenium per year, and the world’s total estimated supply is only about 400 tons.

The main sources of rhenium are the molybdenite and columbite ores. Some rhenium is recovered as a by-product of the smelting of copper sulfide (CuS) ores. Molybdenum sulfide (MoS2) is the main ore and is usually associated with igneous rocks and, at times, metallic-like deposits. Molybdenite is found in Chile, as well as in the states of New Mexico, Utah, and Colorado in the United States.

Hazards

Rhenium is flammable in powder form. Rhenium dust and powder and many of its compounds are toxic when inhaled or ingested.
